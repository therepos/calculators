Historical Financials

Enter the company's most recent year financials. These serve as the base year for forecasting.

DCF valuation begins with understanding a company's current financial position — revenue, expenses, and capital structure.
Why historicals? Past financials reveal trends in revenue growth, margins, and capital intensity. They're the empirical foundation for every assumption you'll make in the next steps.
D&A vs. CapEx Depreciation & Amortization is a non-cash expense that reduces reported earnings but doesn't consume cash — it gets added back when computing FCF. Capital Expenditure is actual cash spent on assets, so it gets subtracted.

Growth Assumptions

Set annual growth rates and margins to project the next 5 years. These drive the entire forecast.

In DCF modeling, we typically use Revenue Growth rates to forecast future revenue, then apply margin assumptions to derive expenses and cash flows.
Revenue growth is king In DCF modeling, revenue growth is the primary driver of the entire forecast. Expenses, CapEx, and working capital are then modeled as percentages of revenue — this is called margin-based forecasting.
Tapering growth Growth rates typically decline over the forecast period. A company growing at 15% today is unlikely to sustain that for 5 years. Tapering from 8% → 4% (as defaulted here) reflects a maturing business.
Working capital (ΔWC) As revenue grows, companies need more cash tied up in inventory and receivables. The change in working capital captures this cash drain — it's often overlooked but materially affects FCF.

Reading the table Revenue flows down: subtract COGS for Gross Profit, subtract SG&A for EBITDA, subtract D&A for EBIT (operating income). EBIT is the key input for the FCF formula in the next step.
EBITDA vs. EBIT EBITDA = Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation & Amortization — a proxy for operating cash generation. EBIT = EBITDA minus D&A — the actual operating profit. The Exit Multiple method uses EBITDA; the FCF formula starts from EBIT.

Free Cash Flow & Discounting

Calculate unlevered free cash flows and discount them to present value.

Free Cash Flow (FCF) = EBIT × (1 − Tax Rate) + D&A − Net CapEx − ΔWorking Capital
FCF = EBIT × (1 − t) + D&A − CapEx − ΔWC
WACC explained Weighted Average Cost of Capital blends the cost of equity (what shareholders demand) and cost of debt (interest rate on borrowings), weighted by the company's capital structure. It represents the minimum return the company must earn to satisfy all investors.
Terminal growth rate Used in the Gordon Growth Model. It should approximate long-term nominal GDP growth (typically 2–3%). Setting it higher implies the company will eventually outgrow the entire economy — which is impossible indefinitely.
Why discount? $100 received in 5 years is worth less than $100 today because of the time value of money — you could invest today's $100 and earn returns. The discount factor converts future cash flows to their present-day equivalent.
Gordon vs. Exit — quick rule Use Gordon Growth for stable, mature companies (utilities, staples). Use Exit Multiple for growth companies or sectors with active M&A. See the Guide tab for a deep comparison.

Valuation Summary

From enterprise value to equity value to fair value per share.

Enterprise Value = NPV of FCFs + PV of Terminal Value
Equity Value = Enterprise Value + Cash − Debt
Fair Value/Share = Equity Value ÷ Shares Outstanding
Enterprise vs. Equity Value Enterprise Value is the total value of the business (debt + equity holders). To get Equity Value (what shareholders own), add cash (an asset shareholders can claim) and subtract debt (a prior claim on the business).
The recommendation If Fair Value/Share > Market Price → BUY (stock is undervalued). If Fair Value/Share < Market Price → SELL (stock is overvalued). This assumes your model assumptions are correct — always sanity-check with sensitivity analysis.

DCF Guide & Reference

Everything you need to understand the discounted cash flow valuation method.

What is DCF Valuation?

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) is an intrinsic valuation method. Instead of comparing a company to peers (relative valuation), DCF estimates what a business is worth based on the cash it will generate in the future, discounted back to today's dollars. The core idea: a dollar tomorrow is worth less than a dollar today.

The DCF Process (6 Steps)

1
Gather Historical Financials

Collect 3–5 years of income statement data: revenue, COGS, SG&A, D&A. These establish the baseline trends you'll project forward.

2
Set Growth Assumptions

Revenue growth is the primary driver. Expenses are typically modeled as a percentage of revenue (margin-based forecasting). Growth rates should taper in later years to reflect market maturity.

3
Build a 5-Year Forecast

Project revenue, then derive COGS, SG&A, D&A, and EBIT for each year. This gives you the operating profit trajectory needed for FCF calculation.

4
Calculate Free Cash Flows

FCF = EBIT × (1 − Tax Rate) + D&A − CapEx − ΔWC. This is the cash available to all capital providers (debt + equity) after reinvestment.

5
Calculate Terminal Value & Discount

Estimate what the business is worth beyond Year 5 (terminal value), then discount all cash flows — including the terminal value — back to today using WACC.

6
Derive Fair Value Per Share

Enterprise Value = NPV of FCFs + PV of Terminal Value. Then: Equity Value = EV + Cash − Debt. Finally: Fair Value/Share = Equity Value ÷ Shares Outstanding.

Terminal Value: Gordon Growth vs. Exit Multiple

Gordon Growth Model
TV = FCF₅ × (1 + g) / (WACC − g)

When to use: Mature, stable companies with predictable growth — utilities, consumer staples, large-cap industrials. The company should be growing at or near the economy's long-term growth rate.

Key constraint: The terminal growth rate (g) must be less than WACC, and typically should not exceed long-term GDP growth (2–3%). If g ≥ WACC, the formula produces infinity — which is nonsensical.

Pros: Theoretically pure — it values the company as a perpetuity of growing cash flows. No need for comparable companies.

Cons: Extremely sensitive to small changes in g. A 0.5% shift can move valuation by 20%+. Assumes constant growth forever, which is unrealistic for high-growth or cyclical firms.

Exit Multiple Method
TV = EBITDA₅ × Exit Multiple

When to use: Companies in sectors with active M&A, or where reliable comparable transaction multiples exist — tech, healthcare, financial services. Also preferred when the company's long-term growth is hard to forecast.

Key input: The EV/EBITDA multiple is usually derived from comparable public companies or recent M&A transactions in the same sector. Typical ranges: Tech 15–25×, Industrials 8–12×, Utilities 6–9×.

Pros: Market-grounded — reflects what buyers actually pay. Less sensitive to perpetual growth assumptions. Easier for practitioners to justify.

Cons: Circular reasoning risk — you're mixing intrinsic (DCF) and relative (multiples) valuation. The multiple itself embeds market sentiment, which may be irrational.

Best practice Many analysts run DCF with both methods and triangulate. If Gordon Growth and Exit Multiple give similar results, you have higher confidence. If they diverge wildly, revisit your assumptions.

Common Pitfalls

⚠️
Over-optimistic growth rates

Very few companies sustain 10%+ revenue growth for 5 years. Use analyst consensus or historical averages as anchors, not aspirational targets.

⚠️
Terminal value domination

In many DCFs, terminal value accounts for 60–80% of total enterprise value. This means your valuation hinges on Year 5+ assumptions. Always sanity-check by computing what implied growth rate the terminal value implies.

⚠️
Ignoring working capital

Growing companies tie up cash in inventory and receivables. Omitting ΔWC inflates FCF and overstates valuation.

⚠️
Using the wrong WACC

WACC should reflect the company's capital structure and risk profile. Using a generic 10% for every company ignores the fact that a utility and a startup have very different costs of capital.

⚠️
Confusing EBIT and EBITDA

FCF starts with EBIT (after D&A), not EBITDA. D&A is then added back. Exit multiples use EBITDA. Mixing these up is a common error.

BUY vs. SELL Decision

After computing fair value per share, compare it to the current market price:

📈
BUY

Fair Value > Market Price → The stock is undervalued. The market hasn't priced in the company's true earning potential.

📉
SELL

Fair Value < Market Price → The stock is overvalued. The market is paying more than what the company's cash flows justify.

Important caveat A DCF is only as good as its assumptions. The recommendation depends entirely on the quality of your revenue forecasts, margin estimates, and discount rate. Always use sensitivity analysis (Step 6) to test how robust your conclusion is.
